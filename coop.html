Co-op Experiences

Most of my co-op experience is in the Project Management space relating to Civil Infrastructure, but I am open to any type of experiences moving forward, including experiences in consulting, software, and infrasturucture design.

First Co-op: WSP

During my time at WSP, I was able to work on a wide range of projects. I was in a client facing role, which allowed me to develop my communication skills as well as learn how to interact with clients and deal with any issues that arose.

My range of projects included retail infrastructure, land development and trail works projects. I was assigned to help manage over 10 projects at a time with my team which improved my time management skills.

The most enjoyable part of this term is when I was given the opportunity to advise clients on the financial side of projects. I was able to advise clients on potential partnerships and do this by developing excel spreadsheets with analysis I had done on project financing.

I learned a lot of things on this co-op and I think these can be summed up in the following 10 points.
1. Contracts are never executed the way they're drawn up. Things are different on paper than in real life. Consulting firms need to be ready to make adjustments to help the project go smoothly for the client.
2. Subcontractors and general contractors both think they’re always correct. This can be a major downfall and lead to mistakes and conflicts.
3. The devil is in the details. Know contracts well, so that financials can be managed appropriately.
4. Trust must be earned, and never given. People must prove that they can be trusted, there are people that will try and take shortcuts when they get the opportunity.
5. Uniform leadership is a must for all infrastructure projects. Things fall apart as different parts of projects are managed by different people who aren’t on the same page.
6. Put technical experts in positions where they can apply their skills and be successful. Allow them to do what they do best and not worry about managing a project.
7. Behavioral patterns will emerge from people over time. These can indicate what type of business decisions people will make, and the intent behind those decisions.
8. Micromanagement can frustrate workers. Put people in positions to succeed, and they won’t need to be managed on such a delicate level.
9. Project engineers are often at odds with contractors. These relationships should be carefully maintained.
10. City officials and administrators have a lot of power. Always keep these types of stakeholders up to date on projects and keep them in the loop.

One of my main tasks was to increase the efficienty of analysts by building templates in Microsoft Excel that would automatically evaluate tax amounts. Another way that I increased efficiency was by building scripts in Visual Basic that would complete repetitive tasks that were previously being done manually. This saved a lot of time for analysts as they completed tax assessments.

I was also able to help work on an specialized audit in the mining sector. I worked on this project with a team and was able to help present findings to a Senior Manager. This allowed me to see what goes on in an audit which was a great experience for me.
